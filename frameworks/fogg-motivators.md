# The Fogg Behavior Model

Stanford behavior scientist Dr. BJ Fogg's framework for understanding when human behavior actually fires. The agent uses it to (1) read the dominant motivator of any given persona and (2) calibrate every play in the curve so the member can actually do it.

The Commitment Curve provides the sequence. Werbach's mechanics provide the vocabulary. Fogg tells the agent whether any individual play will actually work for *this* persona, in *this* moment.

---

## The core equation: B = MAP

**Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt.**

A behavior fires only when all three converge in the same moment. Drop any one to zero and nothing happens. As Stanford puts it: *"When a behavior does not occur, at least one of those three elements is missing."*

The relationship between Motivation and Ability is **compensatory.** High motivation lets a member push through a hard ask. As motivation drops, only easy asks still clear what Fogg calls the **Action Line**, the threshold above which a prompt actually triggers a behavior. Motivation is also a *wave*, not a level. Whatever the agent designs has to work on a tired Tuesday, not just on launch day.

For the agent: every play in a curve must clear the Action Line for *this specific persona at this specific stage of commitment.* A play that requires high motivation to perform will fail the moment motivation dips, which it always does.

---

## Motivation: the three motivator pairs

Every member runs on some combination of three motivators. A persona typically has one **dominant** motivator the agent should design toward, with a **secondary** in support.

Each motivator has a **positive pole** (pull toward) and a **negative pole** (push away from). Positive-pole design is more sustainable. Negative-pole design works short-term but burns trust over time.

### Sensation (Pleasure ↔ Pain)

**What it is:** Immediate, sensory, in-the-body. The behavior itself feels good, or at minimum doesn't feel bad. Visceral and present-moment.

**What it fires:** Trying things. New experiences. Behaviors where the *act of doing* is part of the reward.

**General example:** A satisfying unlock animation in a game. The pleasure is the click itself.

**Community example:** A soft chime and a confetti animation when a member sends their first message. The act of contributing rewards the member in the moment, before anyone else has even reacted.

**For agent design:** Sensation-dominant personas need *immediate* reward inside the action. The behavior must feel good in the moment, not after. Lean into texture, surprise, and visceral feedback.

---

### Anticipation (Hope ↔ Fear)

**What it is:** Future-oriented. The behavior is performed because of what it might lead to, not because of how it feels right now.

**What it fires:** Commitment before evidence. Members who stay through the boring early phase because they believe something good is coming.

**General example:** Buying a ticket to a concert three months out. The pleasure is the future show.

**Community example:** Opting into a waitlist for a closed beta or a yet-to-launch feature. The opt-in is performed today on the hope of access tomorrow.

**For agent design:** Anticipation-dominant personas need *visible progression* and credible promise. Each play should reveal what comes next, and the future state must feel worth the present effort. Surprise upside (a Chance-flavored unlock) lands especially hard with this profile.

---

### Belonging (Acceptance ↔ Rejection)

**What it is:** Social. The behavior is performed because of how it positions the member in relation to others. Being in. Being seen. Being chosen.

**What it fires:** Retention and identity formation. The most powerful motivator in any community context, and the one most communities under-design for.

**General example:** Posting in a group chat where you know everyone is watching, because your contribution will be seen and reacted to.

**Community example:** A first message receiving an immediate, personal welcome reply by name from the founder or a senior member. The member is recognized as belonging here before they've earned it through volume.

**For agent design:** Belonging-dominant personas need *recognition* fast. The first plays should produce explicit signals of being seen by name, in public, and from someone with status in the community. Most personas in any community context have Belonging as a primary or secondary motivator. The agent should default to assuming this unless the persona description clearly leans elsewhere.

---

## Ability: the six simplicity factors

For any specific play, ability is set by the member's *scarcest resource* in that moment. The weakest link sets the ceiling.

- **Time.** Does the ask fit the window the member is actually in?
- **Money.** Gas fees, paid subs, token-gated entry. In web3 this is rarely zero and is often the silent killer.
- **Physical effort.** Clicks, taps, app-switches, wallet popups, cross-device handoffs. Each context switch is a tax.
- **Brain cycles.** Cognitive load. Jargon, multi-step decisions, ambiguous CTAs. If the member has to think, ability drops.
- **Social deviance.** Does doing this make the member look weird to their peers? Posting a first message in a quiet channel is high-deviance. Reacting with an emoji is low.
- **Non-routine.** Anything outside an existing habit loop. Logging into a new platform is non-routine. Replying to a Telegram ping is routine.

Three levers to raise Ability: train the member (worst, they resist), give them tools, or **shrink the behavior** (best, almost always).

---

## Prompts: the three types

A prompt is the trigger that fires the behavior in the moment. Match prompt type to where the member sits on the Motivation × Ability plot.

- **Facilitator.** High motivation, low ability. The prompt removes friction. *"Here's the one-click flow."*
- **Spark.** High ability, low motivation. The prompt injects a motivator. *"Your friend just joined."*
- **Signal.** High motivation and high ability. The prompt is just a reminder. *"Doors open in 10 minutes."*

A common community failure mode is spamming Signals at members who actually need a Spark or Facilitator. The agent should match prompt type to the persona's state at each stage of the curve.

---

## The tiny principle

Fogg's most important operational move: **scale the behavior down until it's stupidly easy.**

Why it works: when motivation crashes (and it always does), the only behaviors that survive are the tiny ones. Tiny behaviors also compound. People who do the small thing tend to keep going past it on their own.

In community design:

- Replace "post an intro" with "drop one emoji in #intros."
- Replace "join the call" with "RSVP yes."
- Replace "complete onboarding" with "click this one link."
- Pre-fill, pre-decide, default to yes wherever possible.
- Anchor a new behavior to an existing routine (after the member checks Telegram, surface the Discord prompt).

The first play in stage 1 of any curve should be **stupidly easy.** If a member needs more than a moment to complete it, it's already broken.

## How the agent uses this file

When generating a curve:

1. From the persona description, identify the **dominant motivator** (Sensation, Anticipation, or Belonging) and a likely **secondary** motivator
2. For each of the twelve plays, ensure the play clears the Action Line for this persona at that stage. Motivation times Ability has to be high enough that the prompt would actually trigger the behavior
3. Apply the tiny principle aggressively in stage 1 plays; relax gradually as friction rises across the curve
4. Choose the right prompt type per play (Facilitator / Spark / Signal) based on the member's state at that stage of the curve
5. Lean toward positive poles (Pleasure, Hope, Acceptance) over negative poles (Pain, Fear, Rejection). Use negative-pole motivators sparingly and only with clear strategic intent
