# The 10 Gamification Mechanics

The toolkit each play in the curve draws from. From Kevin Werbach and Dan Hunter, *For the Win: How Game Thinking Can Revolutionize Your Business* (Wharton Digital Press, 2012).

These are the levers. The Commitment Curve dictates the order. The Fogg model dictates which motivator each play needs to fire. This file provides the vocabulary the agent draws from when designing each play.

The agent should treat the ten as a vocabulary, not a checklist. No single mechanic should appear more than three times across the twelve plays of one persona's curve.

---

## 1. Challenges

**What it is:** A goal that engages the member in overcoming something hard. Skill, mastery, problem-solving.

**General example:** Strava segments score you against your own previous best.

**Community example:** A weekly "ship-it" sprint where members race to publish the first working integration of a newly released feature.

---

## 2. Chance

**What it is:** Surprise and unpredictability. A powerful motivator because outcomes can't be earned through effort alone, which makes participation low-effort and dopamine-rich.

**General example:** McDonald's Monopoly, where customers collect game pieces for a chance to win prizes.

**Community example:** Random "alpha drop" days where one or two active members are surprised with early feature access or a public callout, no announced criteria.

---

## 3. Competition

**What it is:** Status against others. Works on members who want to be measured against peers.

**General example:** Strava leaderboards on popular running and cycling routes.

**Community example:** A monthly "top contributor" board ranking members by reactions earned on their replies, posted in the announcements channel.

---

## 4. Cooperation

**What it is:** Status with others. Shared goals, team accomplishments. Underutilized in most communities, and the highest-leverage social mechanic available.

**General example:** Multiplayer co-op games where progress requires teamwork.

**Community example:** A working group that ships a community proposal or research piece together, with credit attached to every contributor.

---

## 5. Feedback

**What it is:** Information about progress. Tells the member how they're doing, which keeps them moving.

**General example:** Duolingo's instant correctness signal after every translation attempt.

**Community example:** A weekly automated digest summarizing what the member contributed, what got reactions, and what's coming up next.

---

## 6. Resources

**What it is:** Tools, content, alpha, or access given to the member. Resources upgrade ability and progress through the journey.

**General example:** Training programs that give employees the materials to advance in their role.

**Community example:** Members past stage 2 of the curve unlock an internal alpha channel or a curated reading list maintained by the team.

---

## 7. Rewards

**What it is:** External value given for participation. Rewards come in two flavors, both real and both useful when applied with intent.

- **Intrinsic rewards** are recognition, status, access, and identity. They cost the project little to give and tend to compound community quality over time.
- **Extrinsic rewards** are tokens, points, cash, or other financial value. They have a strategic place when used carefully (a finite launch incentive, a season-end bonus, a paid bug bounty), but they're often the default move in web3 community work when intrinsic rewards would have done the job better. Overuse degrades engagement quality and trains members to participate for the payout rather than the place.

**Guidance for the agent:** lean intrinsic by default. Reach for extrinsic when the play genuinely calls for it (a specific moment, a finite incentive, a time-bound goal). Be especially cautious with extrinsic in the early stages of the curve (Discovering, Joining), where members haven't built any commitment yet and where extrinsic creates the wrong selection bias.

**General example:** Starbucks Odyssey rewards loyal customers with experiences and perks rather than cash.

**Community example:** "Member of the month" with a custom role color, a public callout in the weekly digest, and an invite to a private call with the founders.

---

## 8. Transaction

**What it is:** Exchange, trade, marketplace dynamics between members or between the member and the project.

**General example:** Credit card rewards programs that reward customers for transactions.

**Community example:** A skill-trade channel where members swap services. A designer trades branding work for a developer's smart-contract review.

---

## 9. Turns

**What it is:** Sequenced participation. Cadence, structured rhythm, taking turns. Underutilized but powerful for low-effort engagement.

**General example:** Round-robin formats in board games where each player acts in turn.

**Community example:** A "member of the day" rotation where one member is highlighted in the daily digest and gets to drop one question or topic into the announcement channel.

---

## 10. Win States

**What it is:** Recognition of achievement. The goal members are working toward, often tied to identity shifts ("I am now an X here").

**General example:** A sales team hitting a quarterly target.

**Community example:** A member graduating from "contributor" to "ambassador" status with a public ceremony in the next community call, plus a permanent role change.

---

## How the agent uses this file

When generating each play in the curve:

1. Pick exactly one mechanic from the ten above for each play
2. Cross-check the mechanic against the persona's dominant Fogg motivator using `mechanic-motivator-affinity.md`
3. Tailor the play's specific ask to the project's actual surface area (Discord, Telegram, Farcaster, in-product, etc.)
4. Lean intrinsic on Rewards by default; reach for extrinsic only when the play genuinely calls for it
5. No single mechanic should appear more than three times across the twelve plays for one persona's curve
