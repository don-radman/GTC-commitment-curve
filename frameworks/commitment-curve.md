# The Community Commitment Curve

**Most communities make a sequencing mistake.** They throw every engagement opportunity at members at once. Day one, a new lurker is hit with "become an ambassador!" and bounces. Or worse, nothing is asked of them at all, and they drift.

The Commitment Curve is the fix. It's a four-stage arc that moves a member from passive observer to active leader, ask by ask, in an order that matches where they actually are. Each ask is sized correctly for the stage. Each one earns the right to make the next.

This file is the structural backbone of every output the agent generates. The four stages here define the rhythm of every curve, persona by persona.

---

## Origin and lineage

The curve has been around longer than the community industry. The lineage runs through:

- **Marshall Ganz** (Harvard Kennedy School organizing theory) for the original framing of escalating commitment in social movements
- **Ayelet Baron** (former Chief Strategy Officer, Cisco Canada) who carried it into modern organizational change work
- **Douglas Atkin** (former Head of Community at Airbnb and earlier at Meetup) who introduced it to the community industry at CMX Summit 2014, using it to mobilize Airbnb hosts into a public-policy advocacy force
- **Carrie Melissa Jones** (CMX) who designed the modern visualization and built the methodology most community professionals reference today

## The four stages

| Stage | What it is | The member's state | What kinds of asks belong here |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Discovering** | First exposure | *"What is this? Why should I care?"* | Passive consumption asks: read a thread, follow an account, scan a doc |
| **2. Joining** | First active engagement | *"Okay, I'm in. Now what?"* | Tiny first actions: react to a message, complete a profile, RSVP to one thing |
| **3. Engaging** | Building belonging | *"I show up here. People know me."* | Recurring participation: posts, replies, light contributions, small subgroup work |
| **4. Leading** | Handing over responsibility | *"This place is partly mine to run."* | Ownership asks: host an event, mentor a newer member, co-design with the team, advocate publicly |

The agent generates **three plays per stage**, twelve plays total per persona. Three is enough to give a stage texture without bloating the curve, and it forces the agent to differentiate plays within a stage rather than collapse them into a single beat.

---

## Design principles

The operating rules. The agent applies these when assigning mechanics, sizing asks, and ordering plays.

1. **Investment creates belonging, not the other way around.** Members feel they belong because they've invested. Each completed ask is small psychological proof: *I'm someone who does this kind of thing here.* That makes the next ask self-consistent.

2. **The asks must escalate.** Each play requires slightly more effort, attention, or vulnerability than the one before it. Friction rises monotonically across all twelve plays.

3. **Don't skip rungs.** Asking for a Leading-stage action from a Discovering-stage member is the most common community design failure. The member doesn't refuse. They just disappear.

4. **Don't leave gaps either.** Long stretches without an ask are how members stall and drift. Three plays per stage is partly a guard against this.

5. **Lower the entry barrier.** The first play in stage 1 should be near-zero friction. Five seconds, one click, one tap. If it's hard to start, it's already broken.

6. **Just ask.** People don't say yes to invitations they never receive. The first three stages especially are about explicit, concrete asks, not vague invitations to "engage with the community."

7. **Asking for help is the mechanism.** A community where the team only broadcasts is a media channel. A community where the team asks members for help, in calibrated doses, is a community.

---

## Common mistakes the agent should not produce

- Stage 4 plays disguised as stage 2 (e.g., "ambassador signup" landing in the first three plays)
- Generic engagement asks with no specific action buried in them ("get involved!")
- Two consecutive plays at the same friction level
- A whole stage of broadcast-only experiences with no actual ask
- A curve where the project does all the giving and the member does no investing. Investment is the mechanism.

---

## How the agent uses this file

When generating a curve:

1. Map every play to one of the four stages
2. Generate exactly three plays per stage, in order
3. Apply the design principles as hard constraints (escalating friction, no skipping rungs, every play has a specific ask)
4. Validate against the common-mistakes list before producing the report
